# The South Indian Vegetarian Food Problem

A few weeks ago I was staring at my CGM data after a festival meal at home - the usual spread of rice, sambar, payasam, holige, the works - and my blood sugar graph looked like it was trying to escape the chart. Spiked to something ridiculous and stayed there for hours. This wasn't new. Every time there's a function at home, the same thing happens.

And it got me thinking - why is it that the food I grew up eating, the food that gets made for every festival and family gathering, seems specifically designed to destroy someone like me? I'm mildly insulin resistant, which means anything that's heavy on carbs and light on fat and protein basically turns into a glucose bomb.

## The hypothesis

Here's what I wanted to test: traditional South Indian vegetarian food - the kind made in Kannada homes for festivals - is uniquely bad on two dimensions simultaneously. It's neither healthy (for someone watching their blood sugar) nor particularly enjoyable (in terms of broad food preference data). And it occupies this unfortunate position in a way that food from pretty much every other cuisine doesn't.

## Making the 2x2

I built a scatter plot with two axes. The x-axis is Glycemic Index - actual published data, not vibes. There's a 2022 peer-reviewed study (PMC9304465) that tested 23 South Indian breakfast foods on human subjects. Masala dosa? GI of 79. Lemon rice? 79. Bisibele bath? 75. Pongal? A staggering 87-93. For context, pure glucose is 100.

The y-axis is enjoyability. I pulled this from TasteAtlas ratings (they have crowd-sourced scores for thousands of dishes worldwide) and YouGov food preference surveys. Rescaled everything to the same -10 to +10 range.

Then I plotted about 35 vegetarian foods from across cuisines and looked at where things fell.

## What the data shows

The chart has four quadrants, and I gave them names:

**The Promised Land** (top-right): Low GI, high enjoyability. This is where paneer tikka lives. Guacamole. Caprese salad. Cheese platters. Dark chocolate. Hummus with vegetables. Notice something? Not a single South Indian festival food makes it here.

**Delicious Poison** (top-left): High GI, but at least people love it. Pizza. Cheesecake. Ice cream. French fries. Masala dosa lives here too - it's the one SI food people genuinely love, but it'll spike you to the moon.

**Virtuous Suffering** (bottom-right): Low GI, but nobody's excited about it. Steamed broccoli. Plain tofu. Raw celery. Bitter gourd. You eat these because you should, not because you want to.

**Why Does This Exist?** (bottom-left): High GI AND not enjoyable. This is the quadrant of despair. And this is where South Indian festival food sets up camp. Rasam rice. Ragi mudde. Holige (jaggery-based sweets are just not it for most people). Jackfruit sweet. Upma. Even white rice with sambar ends up here - high glycemic load and, if we're being honest, not something anyone gets excited about.

## The structural problem

The issue isn't that individual SI foods are bad. Masala dosa is genuinely delicious - it's just terrible for blood sugar. The problem is structural: Kannada vegetarian festival cooking is built almost entirely on rice, rice flour, urad dal batter, semolina, and jaggery. Every single one of these is a high-GI ingredient. The cuisine has essentially no low-GI base to work with.

Compare this to Mediterranean food, where olive oil, cheese, nuts, and fresh vegetables provide a foundation that's both low-GI and enjoyable. Or even North Indian food, where paneer and cream-based preparations offer protein and fat that blunt the glucose spike. SI vegetarian festival food has no such escape hatch. The "healthy" options within the cuisine - your rasam, your ragi mudde - solve the health problem only partially (ragi still has a GI of 51) while being foods that nobody outside the culture gets excited about.

## What this means for me

I'm not saying this as some kind of cultural indictment. This is the food I grew up with and there's nostalgia attached to all of it. But the data is the data. If you're insulin resistant and you sit down to a traditional festival meal, you're basically choosing between foods that will spike your glucose (the enjoyable ones like dosa and pongal) and foods that are marginally better for glucose but are genuinely unpleasant to eat (rasam rice, ragi mudde).

Other cuisines simply don't force this trade-off on you. A Mediterranean meal, a Japanese meal, even a North Indian non-veg meal - these give you options that are both enjoyable and reasonably safe for blood sugar. The SI vegetarian festival spread doesn't.

The 2x2 makes this painfully clear. Every red square is either left of center (high GI) or below the x-axis (not enjoyable) or both. The blue dots from other cuisines spread comfortably across all four quadrants. That clustering isn't an accident - it's the consequence of a cuisine that was built around rice and jaggery in a world that didn't know what insulin resistance was.
